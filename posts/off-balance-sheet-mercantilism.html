Off-Balance-Sheet Mercantilism

The state becomes America's marginal lender to the sectors it deems strategic. Twenty forecasts, 2027 to 2030.

Modern Mercantilism and artificial intelligence are colliding, and the collision has a financing problem. Mercantilism supplies the motive: secure the sectors the state deems strategic. AI supplies the scale: the largest private capital buildout in history, running straight into the two most politically sensitive markets in America, power and shelter. The fiscal position supplies the method.

The standard map of this era has three territories: tariffs, chips, and subsidies. I think the map is missing the territory where the next decade will actually be decided. The most durable channel of American mercantilism is not the tariff schedule or the grant program. It is the credit system. The state is becoming the marginal lender to the sectors it deems strategic, and it is doing so off the fiscal balance sheet, through guarantees, directed credit, equity stakes, and tax-advantaged conduits, because that is the only instrument it can still afford to use at scale.

Three Constraints

Three constraints force this outcome, and their interaction is the causal engine of the forecasts below.

The first constraint is fiscal. The structural deficit runs near 6% of GDP with interest costs compounding and entitlements politically untouchable (F20). A government in that position cannot buy an industrial policy with appropriations. What it can do, almost for free in scored terms, is underwrite one. A loan guarantee books no outlay until it fails. A conduit bond program spends through the tax code. An equity stake acquired in a moment of corporate need costs nothing and pays optionality. When ambition is unlimited and budget authority is not, policy migrates to the instruments that do not score.

The second constraint is legal, and 2026 supplied the natural experiment. In February the Supreme Court ruled that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs, vacating the core of the 2025 trade regime. Within hours, the same tariffs reappeared under Section 122; Section 232 and 301 investigations were launched to make them permanent before the 150-day clock expired. The lesson generalizes far beyond trade: the mercantilist impulse is regime-level, not statute-level. Strike down one authority and the policy migrates to the next, in diminished but durable form (F1), while the unwinding of the old regime is litigated for years (F2) and Congress declines to reclaim its delegated powers (F3). Anyone forecasting this era by handicapping individual legal instruments is modeling the weather and missing the climate.

The third constraint is political. The two most electorally potent anxieties in America, the cost of shelter and the fear of losing the technology race to China (F4), both demand visible state action, and both are sectors where the state's cheapest lever is credit. This is where the framework stops being abstract, because I spend my working days inside one of its case studies.

Housing: The Domestic Front

Mercantilism raises the cost of shelter inputs through tariffed lumber, steel, copper, and appliances, and through immigration enforcement that shrinks a construction workforce roughly a quarter of which is foreign-born. Electoral politics simultaneously demands cheaper housing. That contradiction cannot clear through markets, so it clears through the state balance sheet.

The evidence is already legible if you know where to look. The 2025 reconciliation bill cut the tax-exempt bond financing test for 4% housing credits from 50% to 25%, roughly doubling the subsidized units each dollar of state bond cap can carry; I underwrite these bonds, and the constraint on production has visibly shifted from bond scarcity to soft-funding scarcity (F12). Congress passed the most significant federal housing supply intervention in a generation with 358 House votes, including a first-of-its-kind ban on institutional investors buying single-family homes, which is a domestic capital control by any honest name. The GSEs are being prepared for share sales while remaining in conservatorship, an arrangement that sells the upside and keeps the control and the guarantee (F8, F9). A formal housing emergency remains on the table (F14).

The ten-year trajectory: private single-family supply stagnates under input-cost and rate pressure (F13), state-subsidized multifamily takes a record share of production, shelter disinflation arrives through the subsidized pipeline rather than through market abundance (F15), and America builds a European-style social-housing finance channel without ever calling it that.

Compute: The Strategic Front

Compute is converging on the same architecture from the opposite direction. The hyperscaler buildout, running at $600B to $700B a year, is industrial policy conducted on private balance sheets: too strategically framed to be allowed to fail, too large to be funded from cash flow. Capex has crossed above free cash flow, and the funding gap is migrating into debt markets at extraordinary speed: hyperscalers' weight in the investment-grade index roughly doubled in a single year, AI-related debt issuance passed $200B in 2025, and datacenter securitization is projected to reach $30B to $40B annually against a refinancing wall estimated near $300B (F17).

Here is where my view differs from both camps in the current debate. The bears, in the Burry tradition, look at this and short everything. The bulls look at demand and underwrite everything. I think both conflate two theses that are about to separate: long the compute, short the structure. Demand for inference is durable, the capacity is preleased years forward, and the constraint is electrons, not enthusiasm (F16, F19). But the liability structures funding the buildout are not durable: GPU-collateralized debt with seven-year collateral inside facility financings written against thirty-year assumptions, single-tenant concentration, neocloud coupons at 9% to 12.5%. Some of that paper breaks within this forecast window (F18).

And when it breaks, the resolution will not be liquidation. It will be absorption. Power bottlenecks are already pulling federal credit toward the sector, and a strategic-asset default would accelerate it (F11). The state becomes the marginal lender to compute for the same reason it became the marginal lender to housing: because the alternative is politically intolerable and the guarantee is fiscally invisible.

The Same Play, Going Global

States that can still afford on-budget industrial policy are running the play openly: sovereign AI factories, national compute funds, and public financing packages for model infrastructure are proliferating across the G20 (F6). Washington, meanwhile, extends control without spending by moving the export-control perimeter from the chip to the computation itself, regulating who may rent frontier compute rather than only who may buy hardware (F5). And the pressure produces adaptation rather than surrender: Chinese domestic accelerators reach commercial scale under constraint (F7), which locks in the competition on both sides. Permanent strategic emergency is permanent justification for state credit.

What the Regime Produces Over a Decade

Follow the causal chain to its endpoints and the next ten years look like this: spreads in state-adjacent credit compress below what fundamentals justify, because the market prices the absorption before it is announced. Contingent liabilities accumulate quietly while measured deficits understate the state's true exposure (F20 is a floor, not a ceiling). Equity-stake capitalism is normalized transaction by transaction (F10) until the U.S. government is a top-ten shareholder across the strategic economy without any legislature ever having voted for that outcome. Losses, when they arrive, are fiscalized rather than recognized, and the ultimate cost surfaces not as default but as term premium and inflation.

Modern Mercantilism's American form will not be state ownership of the means of production. The state will own the liabilities.

What Would Falsify This

Three observations would break the framework, and I commit to them in advance. If both GSEs are fully released from conservatorship with the guarantee genuinely severed (F9 resolving no, in the direction of a clean exit), the state is exiting the credit channel rather than entrenching in it. If 4% LIHTC production is flat through 2027 despite a doubling of effective bond capacity (F12 resolving no), the housing nationalization thesis is overstated and the constraint is real resources, not policy design. And if a $5B+ compute credit event is allowed to liquidate with no federal credit response (F18 yes, F11 no, resolved in that order), then Washington's tolerance for strategic-sector losses is higher than I believe, and the absorption thesis fails. I would rather be wrong precisely than right vaguely.

Analytical Appendix

Calibration method

I assigned probabilities using four inputs: the current base rate, the direction and speed of the observable trend, the incentives of the decision-makers who control the outcome, and the number of independent ways the forecast can fail. I treated 50% as genuine uncertainty, not rhetorical caution. Forecasts above 75% have both a strong structural mechanism and multiple routes to resolution. Forecasts near 50% require a specific political or financial threshold and are more fragile. Three forecasts are deliberately held below 50% as calibration statements rather than hedges: F3 (12%) because Congress reliably complains about delegated tariff power and reliably declines to take it back; F13 (35%) because the consensus overweights the political desire for lower mortgage rates and underweights the inflation regime; F18 (45%) because sponsor equity cushions and hyperscaler absorption of distressed assets can delay recognition past the window even where the structural fragility is real.

The forecasts are intentionally correlated because they test one framework, but they are not duplicates, and the clusters can fail independently. Internal consistency worth noting: F8 (60% share sale) and F9 (80% no full exit) are compatible because the modal world is shares sold inside conservatorship. F16 (capex grows) and F18 (a structure breaks) are compatible because demand strength and liability fragility are independent theses. F13 (rates stay high) and F15 (shelter disinflates) are compatible because the two series run on different clocks. F18 and F11 are sequenced: a compute credit event makes the federal credit response more likely, not less.

Trade and the legal architecture (F1 to F4)

The February 2026 Supreme Court ruling vacated the IEEPA tariffs, and the administration re-imposed a global tariff under Section 122 within hours while opening Section 232 and 301 investigations to make the regime durable before the 150-day clock expired. The revealed preference is that tariffs are regime-level policy, not statute-level policy; the rate ratchets down slowly through litigation, not to zero (F1). The Court left refund mechanics to lower courts, the administration has signaled it will litigate, more than 1,000 tag-along suits are pending at the Court of International Trade, and the government has every fiscal incentive to slow-walk; refunds at scale are a 2028-and-later story (F2). Both chambers have passed disapproval resolutions, but a veto-proof supermajority against a president's core economic program is a different animal (F3). Export controls on rare earths, battery materials, and magnets are now China's standing retaliation currency and its highest-leverage tool; the escalation loop is self-reinforcing on both sides (F4).

The global collision (F5 to F7)

Physical chip controls are incomplete if restricted actors can rent equivalent compute remotely, and U.S. policy has already moved toward customer identification and reporting for infrastructure-as-a-service providers; F5 assumes the control perimeter migrates from the chip to the computation itself. Sovereign AI is not autarky, it is controlled interdependence: local hosting, local keys, auditable operators, and state leverage over capacity, with Europe's AI Factories and InvestAI facility as the template; F6 predicts direct public financing of the AI stack spreads across the G20, which is the international expression of the state-as-lender thesis. U.S. controls exploit real chokepoints and can raise China's cost and slow its frontier, but the same pressure guarantees state demand, financing, talent concentration, and political tolerance for uneconomic early production; F7 uses a commercial-volume threshold rather than a benchmark score because industrial capability is the relevant mercantilist outcome. A yes on F7 would not imply parity: an inferior-but-sufficient domestic accelerator, deployed in larger clusters with lower utilization standards, is strategically adequate. A no would indicate allied chokepoints proved more durable than China's ability to substitute.

The state credit channel (F8 to F11)

The administration wants the GSE monetization, banks have pitched it, and an executive order has already directed FHFA to revisit capital requirements to make the math work; the obstacles (a roughly $200B capital shortfall per congressional testimony, rate sensitivity, litigation) are why F8 sits at 60 rather than 80. The pairing with F9 is the point: the equilibrium is selling equity upside while retaining government control and the implied guarantee. Privatize the upside, nationalize the control. A true release requires resolving capital, the senior preferred stack, and the guarantee question, and no actor with power benefits from finishing that job quickly. On F10, Intel, MP Materials, Lithium Americas, and Trilogy Metals established the template, and critical-minerals policy, CHIPS conversions, and defense-adjacent deals keep adding names; each transaction lowers the political cost of the next one. On F11, power is the binding constraint on the strategic buildout, the buildout is framed as a national security race, and the DOE credit machinery already exists; when private capital hesitates at the power bottleneck, the state's cheapest tool is its balance sheet.

Housing: the domestic front (F12 to F15)

The 2025 reconciliation bill cut the bond financing test from 50% to 25%, which roughly doubles the subsidized units producible per dollar of state volume cap, and state caps were heavily oversubscribed going in. From my underwriting seat, the binding constraint has visibly shifted from bond cap scarcity to soft-funding scarcity and equity pricing, which throttles but does not stop the surge; F12 is the single best real-time indicator of housing's quiet nationalization. F13 is deliberately contrarian: sub-5.50% requires either meaningful disinflation plus cuts, or MBS spread compression from a credible guarantee resolution, and neither is likely inside 18 months after tariff passthrough and the energy shock turned the Fed hawkish. F14 is a genuine coin flip: Treasury floated the emergency publicly and affordability is the top midterm issue, but the IEEPA ruling made emergency-power adventurism legally expensive and the ROAD to Housing Act may have absorbed the political demand for a gesture. F15 exploits a timing wedge: shelter CPI is a lagged average of past rent growth, the 2023 to 2025 multifamily supply wave is still feeding through to new leases, and headline inflation can reaccelerate on tariffs and energy while shelter mechanically decelerates. The components are on different clocks.

Compute: the strategic front (F16 to F19)

The bear case for F16 is a 2027 digestion year, but commitments are contractually locked years forward, preleasing is running in the mid-70% range against a historical norm near 45%, and the constraint is power and construction capacity, not willingness to spend; capex growth slows, it does not go negative. On F17, issuance ran roughly $27B in 2025 with sell-side projections of $30B to $40B annually for 2026 and 2027, and the refinancing takeout need is estimated near $300B against today's much smaller market; hyperscaler capex now exceeds free cash flow, so the funding must migrate into structured markets. F18 is the short-the-structure leg: neoclouds are borrowing at 9% to 12.5% against GPU collateral with roughly seven-year useful lives, stacked inside facility debt written against 20 to 30 year assumptions, with single-tenant concentration and merchant power exposure. These are 2006-vintage structural sins wearing new clothes. The demand thesis and the liability thesis are different theses, and F18 is where they separate. F19 is where the AI buildout meets the median voter: Ohio, Georgia, and Texas have already moved toward dedicated large-load treatment, and every capacity auction that clears at a record transfers the cost fight to state legislatures. Modern Mercantilism is not only national; states are running their own versions against the hyperscalers.

The fiscal anchor (F20)

The structural deficit sits near 6% of GDP with entitlements and interest untouchable, tariff revenue was roughly halved by the Court, and IEEPA refunds are a contingent liability on top. This is the load-bearing fact of the entire framework: a state that cannot afford on-budget industrial policy will conduct it off-budget, through credit.

Scenario map

Off-balance-sheet absorption (base case), 55%. The state credit channel entrenches in housing and compute. Subsidized multifamily takes record production share, GSE shares are sold inside conservatorship, federal credit reaches the power bottleneck, and state-adjacent spreads compress. Contingent liabilities build quietly.

Fiscalized reckoning, 20%. A large compute credit event or a GSE misstep forces an explicit on-budget response. The guarantee becomes visible and priced, term premium rises, and the political fight over who absorbs strategic-sector losses arrives early.

Clean exit, 15%. The falsification world: both GSEs are released with the guarantee genuinely severed, private markets fund the buildout without federal credit, and LIHTC production disappoints despite doubled bond capacity. The state retreats from the credit channel.

Fragmentation shock, 10%. A geopolitical rupture expands export controls and retaliation faster than replacement capacity can be built. The credit channel still grows, but under crisis terms, with inflation doing the fiscal work.

What would change my mind

Beyond the three falsifiers committed to above (F9, F12, and the F18/F11 sequence), I would revise the global cluster down if the U.S. and China negotiate durable compute-access rules or the export-control perimeter stabilizes at physical hardware (cuts F5), if sovereign AI programs stall at announcement without appropriated financing (cuts F6), or if Chinese domestic accelerator output visibly plateaus below commercial scale (cuts F7, and strengthens the case that chokepoint leverage is durable). I would revise the housing cluster if soft-funding gaps fully offset the bond-test expansion in state pipelines, and the compute cluster if preleasing rates fall toward historical norms, which would signal the demand thesis weakening before the liability thesis matters.
