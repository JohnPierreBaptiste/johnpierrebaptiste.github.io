Private Credit is Eating the World and Nobody is Paying Attention

Last fall I sat across the table from a CFO of a mid-market industrial company in Ohio. Good business. Seventy million in revenue, healthy margins, thirty-year track record. He told me his bank had just pulled his revolving credit facility. Not because anything was wrong with the company. Because the bank's internal risk models had been recalibrated post-SVB and his sector no longer fit the box.

He wasn't angry. He was confused. He'd banked with these people for eleven years.

Within six weeks he had a new facility. Same terms, slightly higher spread. The lender was a private credit fund out of Connecticut that he'd never heard of. The process was faster than anything he'd experienced with the bank. He signed the docs on a Thursday and had the money on Monday.

That interaction, multiplied a few hundred thousand times across the economy, is the story of our era. And almost nobody outside of institutional finance is talking about it.

The Numbers Are Staggering

Private credit AUM globally now exceeds $2.1 trillion. For context, that's larger than the entire high-yield bond market. It was $500 billion a decade ago. The growth trajectory doesn't look like a trend. It looks like a phase transition.

But the number that really matters isn't AUM. It's the dry powder figure, the capital raised and committed but not yet deployed. That sits around $450 billion. Which means there are hundreds of billions of dollars actively hunting for borrowers right now, with fund managers under pressure to put that money to work before their deployment periods expire.

Think about what that means for pricing discipline.

I've been watching credit markets professionally for long enough to know that the most dangerous sentence in finance is "we need to deploy." It's the fuel for every deterioration in underwriting standards that has ever preceded a credit cycle turn. And yet here we are, with an enormous and growing pool of capital whose managers are compensated on deployment, not on avoidance of loss.

How We Got Here

The standard narrative goes like this: post-2008 bank regulation (Basel III, Dodd-Frank, various stress testing regimes) made it uneconomical for banks to hold certain loans on their balance sheets. Private credit stepped into the gap. Simple supply and demand.

That narrative is true but incomplete. It misses three things.

1. The death of relationship banking

Banks used to underwrite borrowers based on relationships. Your banker knew your business. He visited your factory floor. He understood that a bad quarter didn't mean a bad company. That model is gone. It was replaced by centralized credit committees, standardized risk scoring, and portfolio-level optimization. When you turn lending into a portfolio construction exercise, you inevitably create gaps. Borrowers who are perfectly creditworthy but don't fit the model. Private credit funds, with their smaller teams and more flexible mandates, can actually do what banks used to do. They can underwrite the borrower, not the category.

2. The institutional appetite machine

Pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, and insurance companies are all structurally short yield. They have long-duration liabilities and a world that, even with higher rates, doesn't generate enough safe income to meet their obligations. Private credit offers them something irresistible: equity-like returns with debt-like volatility. Or at least, that's what the pitch decks say. The fact that the low volatility is partly an artifact of infrequent mark-to-market (you can't have volatility if you don't price the asset) is a conversation most allocators would rather not have.

3. The speed advantage is real

I keep coming back to that CFO in Ohio. He got his facility in six weeks. A comparable bank process would have taken four to six months, involved multiple layers of credit committee approval, and required him to open four ancillary accounts he didn't need. Private credit funds can move fast because they have one decision-maker, or at most a small investment committee. In a world where speed of capital is a competitive advantage, this matters enormously.

The Part Nobody Wants to Discuss

Here is where I'm going to lose some friends.

Private credit has a transparency problem. Not a fraud problem, not a competence problem. A transparency problem. And in credit markets, transparency problems have a way of becoming solvency problems when the cycle turns.

Consider the basic structure. A private credit fund raises capital from LPs. It deploys that capital into bilateral loans to companies. Those loans are not traded on any exchange. They are not rated by the major agencies in most cases. They are valued by the fund manager, sometimes with the help of a third-party valuation firm, but ultimately at the manager's discretion. The LP sees a quarterly report with a NAV that moves smoothly upward, occasionally ticking down a few basis points when a borrower has trouble.

Compare this to the leveraged loan market, where loans trade daily, prices are observable, and you can see in real time when a sector or a borrower is under stress. In private credit, you often don't know there's a problem until the fund manager tells you there's a problem. And fund managers, being human beings with management fees to protect and future fundraises to consider, are not always in a hurry to deliver bad news.

I want to be careful here. I am not saying private credit managers are dishonest. Most of them are doing good work and deploying capital responsibly. What I am saying is that the structure itself creates information asymmetries that, in a stress scenario, could produce very ugly surprises.

The Contagion Channels

If you think private credit is a niche corner of finance that only matters to the institutions invested in it, I'd ask you to reconsider.

Channel one: insurance companies. A massive amount of private credit capital is now sourced from insurance company balance sheets, particularly those affiliated with or owned by alternative asset managers. This is the Athene-Apollo model, replicated across the industry. Policyholder assets backing annuity obligations are being allocated to private credit at scale. If those credit portfolios experience meaningful losses, the backstop is ultimately the state insurance guarantee system. Which is to say, it becomes everyone's problem.

Channel two: banks are coming back in through the side door. After years of ceding ground to private credit, major banks are now partnering with private credit funds through various structures. They originate loans and sell them to funds. They provide leverage facilities to funds. They co-invest alongside funds. The separation between "regulated banking" and "unregulated private credit" is far less clean than the regulatory framework assumes.

Channel three: the borrowers themselves. Private credit is now the marginal lender to a huge swath of the middle market. If private credit funds face redemption pressure or pull back lending, these companies don't have an easy alternative. The banks that used to serve them have dismantled the infrastructure to do so. You could see a genuine credit crunch in the middle market even if the banking system is technically healthy.

What I Got Wrong, and What I Think Now

Three years ago I was more bearish on private credit than I am today. I thought the rapid growth was a classic late-cycle phenomenon and that the correction would come quickly. I was wrong on timing, and I was wrong on something more fundamental.

The shift to private credit is not cyclical. It's structural.

Banks are not coming back to middle-market lending in any meaningful way. The regulatory incentives point the other direction. The technology and talent have migrated. The borrower relationships have been rebuilt. You cannot unscramble this egg.

What this means, practically, is that private credit is becoming a permanent and essential part of the financial system's plumbing. It's no longer an alternative. It's infrastructure. And we should think about it, and regulate it, accordingly.

But "structural" does not mean "safe." The internet was a structural shift too, and it still produced a spectacular bubble and crash before settling into its role as the backbone of the modern economy. The question is not whether private credit belongs. It does. The question is whether the current vintage of deployment, at current spreads, with current underwriting standards, will produce acceptable returns. And on that question, I have real doubts.

Spreads are too tight

Direct lending spreads to the middle market have compressed by 150 to 200 basis points from their 2022 peaks. Some deals are getting done at SOFR plus 475, which for an illiquid, unrated, bilateral loan to a company with $30 million in EBITDA is, in my view, inadequate compensation for the risk. The competitive pressure from all that dry powder is real, and it's showing up in pricing.

Documentation is loosening

Five years ago, the pitch for private credit was: "We get better terms than the syndicated market. Tighter covenants, better protections, more control." That was true then. It's less true now. I've seen recent deals with covenant packages that are barely distinguishable from broadly syndicated loans. The borrower-friendly drift that hollowed out the leveraged loan market is now happening in private credit. It just hasn't been tested yet.

The denominator effect is lurking

Many institutional allocators are now at or near their target allocation to private credit. Some are over. If public markets sell off and the denominator shrinks, these allocators will be forced to slow new commitments or, in some cases, seek liquidity from existing positions. The secondary market for private credit fund interests is growing but still thin. Forced selling into a thin market is not a recipe for orderly price discovery.

So What Do You Do?

I'm not in the business of giving investment advice in blog posts. But I can share how I'm thinking about positioning.

First, I'm differentiating aggressively between managers. In a market this large and this competitive, manager selection matters more than asset class allocation. The difference between a top-quartile and bottom-quartile private credit manager, in terms of realized losses, is going to be enormous over the next five years. Probably the widest dispersion we've seen in the asset class.

Second, I'm paying close attention to vintage diversification. Capital deployed in 2024 and 2025, at tight spreads and loose terms, is not the same risk as capital deployed in 2022 when lenders had real pricing power. Treating "private credit" as a monolithic allocation is a mistake. The question is always: what did you buy, and what did you pay?

Third, I think the most interesting opportunities are at the edges. Asset-backed lending. Specialty finance. Situations that require domain expertise that generalist direct lenders don't have. The core middle-market direct lending space is crowded and commoditized. The edges still offer genuine alpha, if you know where to look and have the operational infrastructure to manage the complexity.

And fourth, I'm watching the insurance channel very carefully. If there is a systemic issue hiding in private credit, that's where I think it surfaces first. The combination of leverage, duration mismatch, and the opacity of the underlying portfolios makes that corner of the market the most fragile.

The Bigger Picture

What fascinates me about the private credit story is that it's really a story about the slow, quiet restructuring of American capitalism's financial architecture. We are moving from a system where banks intermediated between savers and borrowers to one where asset managers do. The implications of that shift are profound and mostly unexamined.

Banks, whatever their flaws, operate within a regulatory framework designed to prevent systemic crises. They have capital requirements, liquidity requirements, stress tests, and deposit insurance. They have examiners who show up and go through their books. Asset managers operate with almost none of that infrastructure. They are regulated as fiduciaries to their investors, not as systemically important institutions.

That framework made sense when asset managers were buying stocks and bonds on behalf of wealthy individuals. It makes considerably less sense when they are functioning as the primary credit providers to tens of thousands of American companies.

I don't know exactly how this resolves. Maybe private credit performs beautifully through the next downturn and the concerns turn out to be overblown. Maybe the regulatory framework evolves gracefully to match the new reality. History suggests neither of those outcomes is particularly likely, but I've been surprised before.

What I do know is that we are running a very large experiment, in real time, with real companies and real capital, and the results won't be fully visible until it's too late to change course. The CFO in Ohio got his money. That's good. The question is what happens when a thousand CFOs like him need to refinance simultaneously into a market that has lost its appetite.

Nobody is paying attention to that question yet. They will.
