An Afternoon at Berkeley

I spent a Saturday at Berkeley. I did not go there. I went to a different elite school in a different major city, the kind with a real sports program and a campus full of the prettiest women in the country, and I will not be naming it because the internet has a long memory and I am writing this under a name that is not quite my own.

But I was at Berkeley, because some friends of mine are there, and they ditched me for an hour to handle something, and an hour alone on a foreign campus with nothing on the calendar is the kind of thing that turns a man reflective whether he wants to be or not.

The Girls and the Chuds

First impression, because I am not going to pretend I was thinking about epistemology in the first ten minutes: there were more attractive women than I expected. I had been told the Berkeley girl was a particular type. Granola, glasses, no makeup, opinions about hummus. There was some of that. But there was also a real fraction of women who would have done perfectly fine on Greek row at any school in the country. Surprising, in a good way.

Less surprising: a lot of chuds. The proportions felt about right for what the school is famous for. Heavy on the engineering and sciences crowd, plenty of guys who looked like they had not seen direct sunlight in two semesters. Nothing wrong with any of it. Just different from what I knew.

The Posters

I have not been on a college campus in a few years and I forgot how loud the wall noise is. Berkeley, predictably, is louder than most. Posters about Palestine. Posters about free speech. Posters from groups I had never heard of advocating for things I did not entirely understand. A guy on Sproul with a microphone yelling about something, and a small group of students yelling back.

The thing I noticed is that almost all of it was coming from one direction. The posters and the speeches and the chalk on the walkways were uniformly coming from the far left of the actual American political distribution. Some of it I agreed with. Some of it I have always agreed with. I am moderate by temperament and slightly liberal by reflex, and even I felt like I was watching a conversation where one side had been politely asked to sit out.

I do not think this is a Berkeley problem specifically. I think it is the entire elite university system, and Berkeley just wears it on its sleeve more than most. Stanford pretends. The school I went to pretends. Berkeley puts up a flyer.

My Dad on Stanford

I called my dad later that night. He went to Stanford in the eighties. I asked him what the cultural difference between the two schools was when he was there.

His answer was that Stanford was, even then, the school of the children of the elite. More conservative on average, in the country club sense of the word, less so in the strict political sense. Berkeley was the school of smart kids whose parents could not write the check. He framed it as the same dynamic that exists between USC and UCLA on the other end of the state. One school full of kids who paid to be there. One school full of kids who tested in.

That dynamic has not entirely held up. UCLA is no longer cheap. Stanford is, for reasons I do not fully understand, drifting in unpredictable directions. But the cultural residue is still there. You can feel it within twenty minutes of walking either campus.

Pretending to Go to Stanford

A few days earlier, I had walked through Stanford. I did the cliche. I went up the tower, paid whatever it costs now, and stood at the top staring at the entire South Bay laid out beneath me. The view is genuinely one of the best I have seen at any university anywhere in the world, and that includes a few in Europe with a head start of several centuries.

I will admit something embarrassing. I LARPed as a student the entire time I was there. Walked through the engineering quad with the right pace. Nodded at undergrads. Looked mildly bored in the right places. People bought it completely. A few asked me what I was studying. I gave a confident answer. There is a particular feeling, walking across a campus you do not actually belong to while everyone assumes you do, that I cannot quite describe except to say it is closer to theater than fraud. The architecture does most of the work. Stanford was built to make you feel like you belong to something larger than yourself, and it does a remarkably good job at it whether you are paying tuition or not.

Who Cleans This Place

Here is the thought I keep coming back to, and the one I cannot quite let go of.

Walking through Berkeley, I started counting the people maintaining the campus. The groundskeepers. The custodians inside the libraries. The men hauling food crates into the dining halls. Almost all of them, on the day I was there, were Latino. Almost all of them looked like they had been doing the same job for a long time. They were the reason the brick walkways did not have a single gum stain on them. They were the reason the bathrooms in Doe Library did not smell like bathrooms anywhere else.

I do not know whether their children get to go to Berkeley. I would guess most do not. The acceptance rate is something like eleven percent and tilts hard toward kids who had the resources to spend four years of high school being optimized for it. A man who has swept the same hallway for thirty years probably did not have the bandwidth to coach his daughter on the personal essay.

Is that a moral failure on the part of the institution. I am genuinely not sure. Berkeley is a public university. It admits, in theory, on merit. In practice, merit is heavily downstream of how much money, time, and information your parents had. Should the people who keep the place running get a real shot at sending their kids inside it. I think the honest answer is that we have already decided no, quietly, and we are very careful not to say it out loud.

I am not arguing for a quota. I am arguing that elite universities do not look at this honestly, because looking at it honestly would force a conversation nobody at the top of the pyramid wants to have.

The White Knights

The other thing I noticed, because I cannot help it, is the particular flavor of student who has clearly decided his role on campus is to advocate, loudly and visibly, for groups he does not belong to. There is a specific tone of voice. There is a specific kind of sweatshirt. There is a specific way of leaning into a megaphone.

I am not going to pretend I do not roll my eyes at it. I do. But some fraction of these kids are going to grow into actually useful adults who fund actually useful things, and the performative phase is the price of admission to that arc. So I try to be patient about it. Sometimes I succeed.

Is It Even Worth It

The deeper question, the one Berkeley actually raised in me as I walked back across campus to find my friends, is whether it still makes sense to spend four years and several hundred thousand dollars on a place like this.

The answer depends on which version of you shows up.

If you are the kid I saw on the third floor of Moffitt, head down, three textbooks open, headphones on, no eye contact for what looked like a six hour stretch, then I am not sure the marginal value of being at Berkeley specifically, over a respectable state school, is what it used to be. The information is on the internet. The credential matters less every year. The grind is the grind whether you do it in Berkeley or in Tempe.

But if you are the other kind of student, and this is the part nobody wants to admit out loud, then a campus like this is one of the last places in American life where you will be densely surrounded by ambitious, smart, slightly weird people for four uninterrupted years. The asset is not the lectures. The asset is the population.

I am a connector by nature. In the one hour my friends left me on Sproul, I talked to fifteen different people. A guy selling something. Two girls handing out flyers. A philosophy grad student who turned out to know someone I work with. A homeless man with a very specific opinion about the chancellor. A senior who recognized the school logo on the inside of my jacket and wanted to know if I was a recruiter. The number of people I would have encountered in an hour at my own school, twelve years ago, was probably in the same range. That density of human contact is the actual product these places are selling, and the kid with the headphones in Moffitt is not buying it.

Would I have done well at a nerdier, more introverted version of an elite school. I do not think so. I think I would have been bored inside of a semester and started a company out of frustration, which honestly might have worked out fine, but it would not have been the same education. The school I went to was loud, hot, social, sporty, beautiful. It rewarded the parts of me that already worked. It did not try to retrain me.

Where I Land

The elite college, in 2026, is a strange and contradictory thing. It is more expensive than ever. It is, on the actual merits of the classroom, less differentiated from a good public school than it has ever been. It is politically lopsided in a way that limits what students get to test their ideas against. The cleaning staff and the children of the cleaning staff are still mostly outside the gates.

The asset was never the lectures. It was the population. And the people who treat the four years like a transcript instead of a network are paying full price for half the product.

And yet. There is something about a beautiful campus, a brick walkway swept clean by a man you will never know, the view from a tower you climbed at noon, fifteen strangers in an hour and one of them turns out to matter, the architecture telling you in a thousand quiet ways that you belong to something important. I do not know if any of it is fair. I know it is real, and I know I miss it, and I know I would do the four years over again, exactly the same way, at exactly the same school.

My friends found me on the south side of campus a few minutes later. The man with the broom finished the corner near the library and moved on to the next one. The posters stayed up. The kids in Moffitt did not look up.
