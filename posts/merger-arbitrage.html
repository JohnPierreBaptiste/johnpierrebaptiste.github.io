Merger Arbitrage in a Volatile Regime: Notes from the Field

There is a particular kind of silence in the minutes after a deal breaks. Not the silence of confusion, but of recalibration. Screens still glowing. The spread, which you had modeled at six percent annualized, suddenly repricing to negative thirty. Your position sizing was reasonable. Your thesis was sound. And none of that matters now, because the world shifted and you are on the wrong side of a binary.

I have been running event-driven strategies for long enough to know that 2025 was not normal. But I am starting to wonder if "normal" was the anomaly.

The Regime Change Nobody Priced

For roughly a decade, merger arbitrage was a carry trade dressed in a suit. You bought the target, you shorted the acquirer, you collected a spread that was thin but predictable. Completion rates hovered above ninety percent. Regulatory risk was a line item in your model, not a thesis. The whole enterprise had the intellectual rigor of picking up pennies, but the pennies were reliable and the steamroller was mostly parked.

That world is gone.

What replaced it is something more interesting, more dangerous, and frankly more honest about what this strategy always was: a bet on your ability to handicap political, legal, and corporate psychology under genuine uncertainty. The antitrust environment alone would be enough to reshape the opportunity set. Layer on geopolitical fragmentation, supply chain nationalism, and a Federal Reserve that spent this year reminding everyone that volatility is not a bug but a feature of monetary policy. The result is spreads that are wider than anything I have seen since 2008, deal timelines that stretch like taffy, and a hit rate that punishes laziness with real capital destruction.

I find this environment exhilarating. Which probably says something unflattering about my personality.

Three Observations from the Trenches

1. The spread is not the edge.

Earlier this year I watched a large-cap technology deal trade at a twelve percent gross spread for weeks. On paper, it looked like a gift. A definitive agreement, committed financing, both boards unanimously in favor. The spread was screaming "free money" to anyone running a simple screen.

The problem was that the screen could not read a room. Regulatory sentiment had shifted in ways that the deal's legal advisors were slow to internalize. I spent two weeks doing nothing but reading public comment letters, tracking personnel changes at the relevant agency, and talking to lawyers who actually practiced before the reviewing body. My conclusion was that the probability of a second request was near certainty and that the probability of a successful challenge was meaningfully higher than the spread implied.

I passed on the trade. Six weeks later, the deal was blocked.

The lesson is not that I am clairvoyant. The lesson is that in this regime, the analytical work that generates edge has almost nothing to do with financial modeling. It lives in regulatory anthropology, in understanding institutional incentives, in reading between the lines of consent decrees from three years ago. The spreadsheet is the last step, not the first.

2. Timing risk has become a first-order variable.

In the old regime, a deal that took fourteen months instead of eight was an annoyance. Your annualized return compressed, you grumbled about opportunity cost, and you moved on. In today's rate environment, that same delay is a fundamentally different trade. The carry cost of being wrong about timing, not outcome, can eat your entire spread.

I have started thinking about merger arb positions the way a commodity trader thinks about contango. The shape of the forward curve matters as much as the spot price. If I believe a deal closes but I am wrong about when by six months, is the risk-adjusted return still attractive after financing costs? Often the answer is no. And "no" is an underrated word in portfolio management.

3. The broken deals are where the real money hides.

This is counterintuitive, so let me be precise. I am not talking about buying targets after deals collapse, hoping for a bump or a white knight. That is a different game with its own logic. What I mean is that the fear of deal breaks has created systematic mispricing across the entire merger arb universe. Spreads on perfectly healthy transactions are wider than they should be because allocators and risk managers are flinching at the memory of recent losses.

Fear is a blunt instrument. It does not distinguish between a deal facing a serious antitrust challenge and a deal where the regulatory path is straightforward but happens to be in the same sector as last quarter's blown-up trade. This creates opportunities for anyone willing to do genuinely differentiated work on the regulatory dimension rather than relying on historical base rates that no longer apply.

What This Means for Market Structure

The wider implication is that merger arbitrage is becoming a real alpha strategy again, after years of functioning as a quasi-fixed-income substitute. The passive merger arb ETFs that proliferated in the low-vol era are going to struggle. They are built on the assumption that diversification across a large number of deals smooths out idiosyncratic risk. But when the risk is regime-level, when the antitrust philosophy of the entire enforcement apparatus shifts, diversification does not save you. It just means you own a portfolio of correlated bets that all go wrong simultaneously.

Active managers with genuine regulatory expertise, real networks, and the willingness to run concentrated books are being handed a structural advantage they have not enjoyed in over a decade. The irony is that many of them shut down or shrank during the years when the strategy was easy and the fees were hard to justify. The survivors are leaner, more disciplined, and playing a game with fewer competitors and wider margins.

A Final Thought

Someone asked me recently whether I thought the current regime was good or bad for markets. I told them it was the wrong question. Volatility, uncertainty, regulatory aggression. These are not problems to be solved. They are the landscape. The only relevant question is whether your process is adapted to the terrain you are actually on, rather than the terrain you remember.

Most people in this business are navigating by a map drawn in 2019. The territory has changed. The ones who noticed are going to do very well.
