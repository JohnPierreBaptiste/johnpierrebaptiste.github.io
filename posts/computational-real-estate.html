The Case for Computational Thinking in Real Estate Underwriting

The average real estate analyst spends 70% of their time doing something a Python script could do in four seconds. They don't know this. They think the spreadsheet is the analysis.

That confusion is going to be expensive.

The Excel Ceiling

Excel is a brilliant tool. It's also a cage. When your entire analytical worldview fits inside a grid of rows and columns, you start to mistake the tool's constraints for the shape of reality. You build a 12-tab waterfall model and feel sophisticated. Meanwhile, the actual question you should be asking, "What happens to this deal under 10,000 different rent growth scenarios?", never occurs to you. Because Excel makes that question feel impossible.

It's not impossible. It's six lines of code.

What Computational Thinking Actually Means

I'm not saying every associate needs to become a software engineer. I'm saying they need to think in systems, not cells.

Computational thinking means asking: What are the inputs? What are the rules? What are the boundaries of the possible outcomes? It means understanding that a cap rate is not a number. It's a distribution. It means recognizing that your "base case, upside, downside" trichotomy is a fiction you invented because your tool couldn't handle the truth.

A Monte Carlo simulation doesn't care about your three scenarios. It shows you the full probability surface. And building one takes an afternoon, not a quarter.

The Arbitrage Is Now

Here's what I find fascinating. The firms that are adopting computational approaches to underwriting aren't hedge funds or tech companies. They're small, scrappy operators who realized that a $200/month cloud computing bill and one curious analyst can outperform an entire acquisitions team running legacy models.

I watched a two-person shop stress-test a 400-unit multifamily deal against granular, unit-level lease expiration data, local employment shocks, and insurance premium volatility. Simultaneously. Their competitors were still toggling between "optimistic" and "conservative" rent growth tabs.

The gap between these two approaches isn't incremental. It's categorical.

The Uncomfortable Part

If you've spent a decade mastering VLOOKUP and circular references, this might feel like a threat. Good. Discomfort is the leading indicator of relevance. The analysts who learn to code, even just a little, will eat the lunch of those who don't. Not because code is magic. Because code forces you to think clearly about what you're actually modeling.

The spreadsheet tells you an answer. The algorithm tells you how wrong that answer might be.

That difference is everything.
