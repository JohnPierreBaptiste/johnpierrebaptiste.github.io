On Vagabonding

Notes from a long trip in progress.

I am writing this from a wifi cafe somewhere I did not know existed three weeks ago, in a city I had no plans to visit when I left home. I am a few months into what is loosely called a sabbatical and more accurately called vagabonding, and I want to write some of this down before the next train.

The route, for anyone keeping score. Dallas. Phoenix. Los Angeles. Mexico City. San Francisco. New York. Then a flight east and a couple of cities in Italy. After that I genuinely do not know. The not knowing is the point. The not knowing might be the only point.

I want to try to explain why.

The Plan That Isn't a Plan

Most people I know who have done a long trip planned every leg. They booked hotels in advance. They had a spreadsheet. They optimized. I respect the impulse. I am not built for it.

The way I have done it is closer to the way an old freight train used to work. You stop in a city. You stay until the city stops giving you anything new. Then you check three flights and pick the cheapest one going somewhere you have never been. That is the entire methodology.

It sounds romantic. It is also, I will admit, occasionally a logistical nightmare. The flight is at six in the morning and the metro does not run yet. The apartment I booked in Mexico City turned out to be on the third floor of a building with no elevator and three locked gates between the street and the front door, none of which my host explained until I had been wandering the block for forty minutes with a duffel on my shoulder. The currency exchange rate moves while you are pulling cash out of the ATM. Somebody charges me for water in Italy and I am still not sure if I should have tipped.

These are the small things. They are also the reason I am doing this.

The Joy of Small Problems

There is a kind of muscle that atrophies in modern professional life. You stop solving small things in person. Your apartment is set up. Your groceries arrive in a paper bag at the door. Your laundry is on a recurring schedule. Your dentist sends you reminders. The texture of the day is curated, frictionless, optimized so that the only problems left for you to solve are the medium-large ones at work.

Travel breaks that. Cheerfully and without permission.

In a typical week on the road I have solved problems my American adult life had insulated me from for years. How to convince a barber in a city where I speak no Spanish that I want a fade and not a full buzz. How to order coffee in a language where I do not have the word for milk. How to find a SIM card on a Sunday in a country where everything is closed on a Sunday. How to navigate a city that does not believe in street signs. How to figure out which of the three identical bus stops is the one for the airport, with eight minutes until the bus arrives.

None of these things are important. Each of them is the best part of my day. Because each of them requires me to be present, alert, willing to be wrong, and willing to ask a stranger for help in a way I never have to ask anyone for help at home.

The currency I am earning from this trip is not memory. It is competence in friction. I am getting better at being slightly lost. That is a real skill, and it is one most adults I know have completely lost.

A Tuesday in Mexico City

I want to pin one of these down, because abstract is cheap and specific is real.

A Tuesday in Mexico City. I had just landed from Los Angeles. The Uber to the apartment did not show up. My data plan had not activated on the new SIM. The apartment host had not responded to my message because his phone, I later learned, was on his other phone, which was at his sister's house. I had a backpack, a duffel, and a vague address. I did not speak the language.

I walked into the closest cafe. I ordered a cortado with the two Spanish words I had. The man behind the counter watched me for a beat, then said, in English, that I looked like I needed help more than I needed coffee. He let me use his wifi. He helped me message the host. He sat with me until I had a confirmed address. He charged me thirty pesos for the cortado, which is about a dollar fifty.

That is the trip. That has been every leg of it. Some specific person, somewhere, who decided I was worth two minutes of their time. I have been in seven cities so far and the through line is not the food, the architecture, or the views. The through line is people. Specific, kind, generally unmemorable in any cinematic way, people.

The Quiet Privilege

I want to be honest about something else.

The reason I get to do this is not that I am especially brave or unusually free. It is that the work I do, the people I work with, and the way our team has structured itself has made this kind of trip possible. I can think and write and contribute from anywhere with a decent connection. I have colleagues who cover the in-room work when I am out of the room. I have an employer that does not require me to perform presence to prove productivity.

That is rare. I do not pretend otherwise.

Everybody I work closely with is in a similarly lucky position. Four of us are in a group chat sharing photos from four different countries this week, and the four of us are an unusual sample of the labor market. The version of this trip I would have run five years ago, when I was on a different team in a different industry, was unimaginable. I was performing seat time. I was at a desk by 8 AM. The trip would have been two weeks of guilt, three weeks of email, and one week of actual presence in another country. Nobody would have called it vagabonding. They would have called it a vacation, and they would have meant it as an insult.

What changed is not me. What changed is the kind of work I do and the kind of place I do it. I am grateful in the specific and unspoken way that adults are grateful for the lucky breaks they did not entirely engineer.

And Yet

Here is the contradiction I cannot tidy up.

I love the road. I also love the work.

I am not the kind of person who travels in order to escape his job. I travel because the work and the wandering are, for me, two parts of the same thing. The wandering loosens the head. The work focuses it. The two of them in alternation, week after week, produce a kind of thinking I do not get from either one alone.

There is a romantic version of this essay that ends with the narrator deciding to quit his job and live on a beach in Mexico forever. I have read that essay. I have respected that essay. I am not writing that essay.

The version I am writing is the one where the trip is great and the job is great and the person doing both, eyes open, is more present in each of them because of the other.

Where I Am Going

I am supposed to fly somewhere in Europe in a week or so. I am not yet sure which city. I will pick when I know which flight is the cheapest that morning and which friend has the best couch open. I will probably stay longer than I should and leave later than I planned.

After that, I genuinely have no idea. I might go back to the apartment I keep half-paying for in a city I have not been to in three months. I might keep going east. There is a part of me that wants to spend a month in Tokyo doing nothing but eating at the same kissaten every morning and reading until the language starts to make sense again. There is a part of me that wants to be back in a real office on Monday.

Both are real. I am not in a rush to resolve the tension. The not knowing, I have to keep reminding myself, is the entire point.

This essay is a work in progress, the way the trip is. I will write the rest of it from somewhere.
