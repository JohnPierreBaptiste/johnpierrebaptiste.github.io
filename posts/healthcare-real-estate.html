The Argument for Healthcare Real Estate in a Post-Rate-Hike World

Last spring I toured a memory care facility outside of Richmond that had been sitting on the market for fourteen months. The building was fine. Clean, well-maintained, 92% occupied. The operator was turning a profit. And yet nobody would touch it, because every generalist investor I know had the same reaction when they heard "skilled nursing" or "senior housing" in 2024 and 2025: they flinched.

That flinch is the entire thesis.

I ended up not buying that particular property for reasons specific to the deal. But the experience crystallized something I'd been circling for over a year. Healthcare real estate, broadly defined, is sitting in a pocket of structural mispricing that has almost nothing to do with the fundamentals of the asset class and almost everything to do with how capital allocators sort the world into neat boxes. When rates went up, real estate went into the "bad" box. Healthcare went into the "complicated" box. The intersection of the two became basically invisible.

I think that's about to change, and I think the people who move now will look very smart in three years.

The Demographic Freight Train

Let's start with what should be obvious but apparently isn't. The United States has roughly 55 million people over 65. By 2030, that number crosses 73 million. By 2034, for the first time in American history, there will be more people over 65 than under 18. These are not projections built on shaky assumptions. These people are already alive. They already have birthdays. The math is settled.

What's not settled is where they're going to receive care.

The existing supply of senior housing, skilled nursing facilities, medical outpatient buildings, and behavioral health centers is nowhere close to adequate for what's coming. New construction essentially froze during the rate hike cycle. Between 2022 and 2025, construction starts for senior housing dropped to levels we hadn't seen since the early 2000s. The pipeline is thin. In many secondary markets, it's nonexistent.

This is the setup that every real estate investor claims to want: constrained supply meeting guaranteed demand growth. And yet when I bring up healthcare real estate at dinners and conferences, I watch people's eyes glaze over. They want to talk about data centers. They want to talk about logistics. They want to talk about anything that feels like the future rather than something that feels like fluorescent lighting and linoleum floors.

I get the aesthetic objection. I just think it's an expensive bias to hold.

Cap Rates: The Dislocation Nobody Talks About

Here's where it gets interesting from a pure numbers perspective. During the zero-rate era, cap rates on core healthcare assets compressed to levels that made the sector genuinely unattractive on a risk-adjusted basis. You were buying a 5.5% cap on a skilled nursing facility in a world where industrial was trading at 4%. The spread didn't compensate you for the operational complexity. Fair enough.

But the rate hike cycle repriced healthcare real estate far more aggressively than the fundamentals warranted. Cap rates on medical office buildings blew out 150 to 200 basis points. Senior housing saw even more dramatic moves. Meanwhile, occupancy was recovering. Rent growth was accelerating. The operating environment was actually improving while the capital markets were punishing the sector.

Why? Because healthcare real estate has a smaller, more specialized buyer pool. When generalist capital retreats from real estate broadly, it retreats from healthcare first and fastest. The sector lacks the deep, liquid bid that industrial and multifamily enjoy. So prices overshoot to the downside in exactly the same way they overshoot to the upside during easy money periods.

Right now, you can acquire stabilized medical outpatient buildings in strong hospital-anchored locations at cap rates north of 7.5%. Senior housing with value-add upside is trading at 8% to 9% going in, with realistic paths to 10%+ stabilized yields. These are numbers that would have been unthinkable three years ago and that I believe will be unthinkable again three years from now.

The window is open. I don't think it stays open long.

The Operator Consolidation Wave

The part of this thesis that I find most underappreciated is what's happening on the operating side. The pandemic and subsequent labor crisis broke a lot of small, undercapitalized operators. They're gone. What's replacing them is a new generation of institutional-quality management companies with better technology, better staffing models, and better data infrastructure.

I spent two days last fall with the leadership team of a regional operator that runs about forty facilities across the Southeast. They showed me their real-time staffing optimization platform, their predictive analytics for census management, their procurement system that was saving them 12% on supplies compared to the mom-and-pop operators they'd acquired facilities from. This isn't your grandmother's nursing home company. These people are running sophisticated operations with margin profiles that would surprise anyone whose mental model of senior care is stuck in 2015.

The consolidation trend matters enormously for real estate investors because operator quality is the single largest determinant of asset-level performance in healthcare. A great building with a bad operator is a money pit. A mediocre building with a great operator can be a cash machine. As the operator landscape professionalizes, the variance in outcomes narrows, and that makes the whole sector more underwritable.

This is the exact dynamic that played out in self-storage fifteen years ago. Fragmented, unsophisticated operators gave way to institutional players. The real estate became more predictable. Capital flooded in. Values re-rated dramatically. I'm not saying healthcare follows the same timeline. But I am saying the parallels are striking and the market isn't pricing them in.

The Labor Question

The obvious pushback is labor. Healthcare is labor-intensive, the workforce is strained, and wage inflation has been brutal. All true. But I'd argue this is priced in twice over and improving at the margin.

Staffing ratios have stabilized. Wage growth in healthcare services has decelerated meaningfully from the 2022 and 2023 peaks. Immigration policy, whatever you think of it politically, is trending toward more openings for healthcare workers. And technology is starting to make a real dent. Not in replacing caregivers, but in reducing the administrative burden that was driving burnout and turnover.

One operator I work with reduced their turnover rate from 68% to 41% over eighteen months primarily through scheduling flexibility and better onboarding technology. That kind of improvement flows directly to the bottom line and directly to real estate performance.

Why Generalists Get This Wrong

I've spent a lot of time thinking about why smart, sophisticated investors consistently misunderstand healthcare real estate, and I think it comes down to three cognitive errors.

Error One: Treating All Healthcare the Same

When most investors hear "healthcare real estate," they think of a single category. But the sector contains wildly different risk and return profiles. A credit-rated hospital ground lease, a Class A medical office building anchored by a health system, a private-pay memory care community, and a Medicaid-dependent skilled nursing facility are about as similar to each other as a Manhattan office tower and a rural self-storage facility. The lumping together of these subcategories into one mental bucket causes people to apply the risk premium of the hardest, most operationally complex assets to the entire sector. That's just sloppy thinking.

Error Two: Overweighting Regulatory Risk

Yes, healthcare is regulated. Yes, reimbursement changes can impact operator economics. But the regulatory environment in healthcare real estate is far more stable than people assume. Medicare and Medicaid reimbursement rates have actually increased meaningfully over the past two years. The regulatory trend, if anything, is toward supporting the existing infrastructure because the alternative is a crisis that no politician wants to own.

Compare this to the regulatory risk in, say, multifamily housing, where rent control legislation is proliferating in ways that are far less predictable. Or the regulatory risk in data centers, where energy policy and permitting are genuine wildcards. Every asset class has regulatory exposure. Healthcare's is just more visible, which paradoxically makes it more manageable.

Error Three: Anchoring to the Pre-Pandemic Experience

The pandemic was devastating for senior housing and skilled nursing. Occupancy cratered. Some operators went under. The images of overwhelmed facilities are seared into the collective memory of the investor class. But anchoring to that experience when making forward-looking allocation decisions is like refusing to fly because you once had turbulence. The pandemic was a once-in-a-century shock. The industry survived it, adapted, and in many ways emerged stronger. Using 2020 as your baseline for healthcare real estate risk is not conservative. It's just wrong.

The Structural Tailwinds Nobody Mentions

Beyond demographics, several less-discussed forces are converging in ways that benefit healthcare real estate.

First, the shift from inpatient to outpatient care continues to accelerate. Every major health system in the country is moving procedures out of the hospital and into ambulatory surgery centers and medical outpatient buildings. This is a multi-decade trend that creates enormous demand for well-located, purpose-built outpatient facilities. These are not commodity assets. They require specific infrastructure, and they generate long-duration, high-credit leases.

Second, behavioral health is experiencing a capacity crisis that is, if anything, more acute than the one in senior housing. Demand for inpatient and outpatient behavioral health services has surged post-pandemic, and the physical infrastructure simply does not exist to meet it. I've seen investors generate returns north of 20% IRR on behavioral health facility development in markets where the permitting environment is favorable. This is a sub-sector that barely existed as an institutional asset class five years ago.

Third, the convergence of healthcare and housing is creating new hybrid asset types that defy traditional categorization. Think medical-adjacent senior living, wellness-oriented communities with integrated primary care, assisted living facilities designed around chronic disease management. These concepts are early but growing fast, and the real estate underlying them will be valued very differently as the models prove out.

How I'm Thinking About Positioning

I'm not a "buy everything" person. Even within a sector I'm bullish on, selectivity matters enormously. Here's how I'm sorting the opportunity set.

Highest conviction: medical outpatient buildings in growing MSAs with strong health system anchors, acquired at cap rates above 7%, with near-term lease rollover that allows you to mark rents to market. The re-leasing spreads in medical office right now are among the best in all of commercial real estate, and almost nobody is paying attention.

Strong conviction: senior housing in supply-constrained markets with improving occupancy trends, particularly private-pay communities where you're not exposed to reimbursement risk. The operating leverage in senior housing is tremendous. The difference between 82% and 90% occupancy on a 120-unit community can be the difference between a 6% and an 11% cash-on-cash return. We're watching occupancy grind higher nationally, and the supply pipeline suggests it will continue.

Opportunistic: skilled nursing facilities with strong operators in states with favorable Medicaid reimbursement trends. This is the part of the sector that scares people most, and therefore where the risk premiums are most extreme. You need to know what you're doing here. You need operator relationships and regulatory expertise. But the entry points available today on well-run SNFs are genuinely extraordinary by historical standards.

What I'm avoiding: anything that requires significant new construction to work, anything in markets with upcoming supply waves, and any deal where the thesis depends on reimbursement rates moving in a specific direction. I want assets where the demographics alone are sufficient to drive returns, with everything else as upside.

The Punchline

Every few years, the market hands you a sector where the fundamentals and the pricing diverge dramatically because of some combination of narrative, complexity, and bad timing. Residential mortgage credit in 2012. Energy infrastructure in 2016. Public REITs in early 2020.

Healthcare real estate in 2026 has that same quality. The demand is as certain as anything gets in investing. The supply response is constrained. The operating environment is improving. And the pricing still reflects a world where rates are going up, COVID is ongoing, and nobody can hire a nurse.

None of those things are true anymore.

The investors who do the work, who learn the sub-sectors, who build the operator relationships, who get comfortable with the complexity. They're going to be rewarded for it. And the ones who keep flinching every time they hear "healthcare" are going to watch from the sidelines while the opportunity reprices in front of them.

I know which side of that I want to be on.
