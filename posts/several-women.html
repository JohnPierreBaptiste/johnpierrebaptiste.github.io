On Wanting Several Women at Once

I am going to say something that offends approximately everyone, so let me draw the lines first. I am not married. I am in my thirties. The women in my life right now include an ex who still texts me on Sunday afternoons, a colleague who finds reasons to come by my desk, three women I met on Hinge over the last six months, and a friend from high school who became something more about a year ago and then quietly stayed there.

I love several of them. Not equally. Not in the same shape. But I love them.

And I cannot pick.

That is the part nobody wants to hear, especially the women themselves. It is not commitment-phobia in the therapy sense. I commit to plenty of things. Funds. Companies. The 5 AM gym. What I cannot commit to is the idea that the next ten years of my life should be allocated to one person while the curve I am on keeps bending up.

The Math Nobody Says Out Loud

The most useful framework I have come across for dating in your thirties is one almost nobody will state in mixed company. A man's value, in the very specific market of who wants to partner with him, generally compounds with time. Your career is more legible. Your accounts are deeper. You have stories. You have stopped doing the dumber half of what you did at twenty-four. The women who want you at thirty-four are, in most measurable ways, more impressive than the women who wanted you at twenty-four.

This is not a flex. It is just the demographic reality of how heterosexual dating works on a long enough timeline.

For women in the same market, the math runs in the other direction. I am not the one who made the rules. I am observing them. You can be furious about it and still it will be true.

So when a man in my position chooses to commit at thirty-two, he is making a quiet bet that the value he is getting today is the highest value he will ever access. That is a strange thing to wager your life on when every signal in the environment, including the woman from Hinge who matched you last week and the colleague who keeps engineering reasons to walk past your desk, suggests the opposite.

I am not saying the math is good. I am saying the math is real.

Elon

It is impossible to write about this honestly in 2026 without writing about Elon Musk. The richest man on the planet has, at last public count, somewhere between eleven and fourteen children with several different women, some of whom were never his romantic partners in any traditional sense. He selects mothers. He invites them into a kind of distributed household. He has reportedly offered women significant money to bear his children. He does not pretend any of this is monogamous.

The interesting thing is not that Elon is doing this. The interesting thing is that nothing happens. He gets richer. The companies keep working. The women keep agreeing. There is a Twitter cycle of outrage every six months and then it dies out, because nobody can articulate which rule he is actually breaking.

Compare that to a low-income man with three children by three women in a different zip code. We have a whole vocabulary of contempt for him. We do not have one for Elon.

The honest read is that monogamy was never a moral position to begin with. It was a resource allocation strategy in a pre-industrial world where one man could not feed thirty children and one woman could not raise them alone. The morality got bolted on top of the economics later, the way it always does. Take the economics away, which is what extreme wealth and modern reproductive technology do, and the morality starts to look like a costume.

Harems

The word harem makes Americans uncomfortable, partly because of its specific historical baggage and partly because we like to pretend our own arrangements were never like that. They often were. Solomon, the wise king of the Old Testament, allegedly had seven hundred wives and three hundred concubines and no preacher I ever heard wanted to deal with that verse on a Sunday morning. The Ottoman sultans built entire architectural complexes to house theirs. The emperors of China formalized the rankings inside them. The Mughal courts. The Aztec elite. Pre-Christian Ireland. The big men of pretty much every successful pre-modern civilization.

What is striking when you read the actual histories is how administratively normal these arrangements were. The harem was not, in most cases, a den of pure sensual indulgence. It was a parallel court. It had budgets. It had political factions. The women inside it had real power, sometimes more than the men outside it. The mother of the sultan was often the most influential person in the empire.

What changed is not human nature. What changed is the ability of one elite man to extract enough surplus from his society to maintain dozens of women and their children at the standard they expected. Industrial capitalism flattened that for a few centuries. Late-stage tech wealth is unflattening it.

Look at Silicon Valley right now. Founders openly discuss having multiple long-term partners. The polyamory scene in the Bay Area is dense with people who pull FAANG salaries, which is not a coincidence. Once you can rent four bedrooms in Atherton without flinching, the constraints that made monogamy practical begin to evaporate. The Berlin scene works the same way. So does Manhattan, quieter, behind doormen.

I am not arguing this is good. I am arguing it is happening, and that the people pretending it is not happening are reading the wrong newspapers.

What I Have Actually Learned

The clean version of this essay would now pivot to a redemption arc. I would tell you that, through some painful incident, I learned what I really wanted was one person and a quiet life. I would tie it up. The reader would feel reassured.

I am not going to do that, because it would be a lie.

What I have actually learned, sleeping with and loving several women across this current chapter, is that the standard cultural script about how men feel about this is largely fiction. I do not feel hollowed out. I do not feel like I am missing something. I feel, on most mornings, like a man who has built a life that finally rewards the effort of the previous decade. The women in it are not interchangeable. They are not even, individually, replaceable. Each of them is a specific person I would mourn losing.

What I have also learned is that the women involved are far more rational about this than the culture admits. The ex still texts because she is also figuring out what she wants and I am a known quantity. The colleague is not naive. She knows. The Hinge dates are doing their own version of the same math. The friend from high school knows me better than anyone alive and chose this anyway. None of them are victims of my charm. They are all making their own bets on their own time horizons.

The honest cost is not what you would expect either. It is not guilt. It is logistics. Calendars. Friend groups overlap. The constant low-grade fear of saying the wrong name. The weeks where you genuinely lose track of who told you what, and you have to write it down somewhere your phone will not betray you.

Where I Land

I do not know if I will ever marry. I used to assume I would, the way you assume you will eventually have a knee replacement. Now I am genuinely uncertain. The question of whether modern marriage, as currently constructed in upper-middle-class America, is a good deal for a man on a rising trajectory has, in my reading, no clean answer.

What I do know is that the official story we are told about love and partnership in 2026 was written for a world that does not exist anymore. It was written for a world in which one income could not support two adults, in which divorce was financially ruinous for women in particular, in which contraception was unreliable, in which a man's economic peak came in his late twenties rather than his late forties, in which the religious and social cost of straying was unbearable.

Almost none of those facts are still true.

Monogamy was an answer to a problem that has, for a thin layer of people in this century, mostly been solved by money and birth control. We are still answering the question as if the problem were the same.

I am not telling anyone what to do with that. I am telling you that the people you assume are quietly living the standard life increasingly are not, and the gap between what people say they want and what they actually arrange is wider than it has ever been in my lifetime.

I love several women. I am going to keep doing what I am doing for now. The math says the curve still bends up. When it stops bending, I will reconsider.

Until then, the texts keep coming on Sunday afternoons, and I keep answering.
