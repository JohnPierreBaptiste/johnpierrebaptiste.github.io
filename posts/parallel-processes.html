Parallel Processes

On AI safety, Welltower, and learning to commit.

There is a residency in Berkeley called Generator. Three months, AI safety generalist track, run by Kairos and Constellation. The application closed on April 27, two days ago. The moment I heard about it, my instinct was to apply.

The instinct is the problem, and I want to try to explain why.

Two weeks ago I signed an offer to start in June as a Senior Analyst at Welltower in New York. I will be sitting on the investments team at one of the largest healthcare REITs in the world, working on the asset class I have spent the last several years building real conviction in. It was the offer I wanted. I took it without hedging. The version of me from six months ago would have told you, with certainty, that this was the seat.

Then Generator showed up. And the first thing I felt was not curiosity. It was something closer to relief, which is the part I want to interrogate.

The Deeper Pattern

In the last eighteen months I have run, by my count, about eight parallel recruiting processes. PE shops. Hedge funds. A growth-stage tech company. A residency. Two REITs. A family office. A consulting firm I had no business interviewing at. In the same window I have started a social polling app, co-founded an AI company in the medical device space, run an affiliate marketing operation, started a YouTube channel, and rebranded my personal site twice.

I have entertained, with what I would have called serious intent, at least four full career pivots. AI safety. Solo software. A PhD. A second stint in banking. Each one arrived dressed in high-agency clothes. Each one felt, in the moment, like the obvious next move.

If you had asked me at any point in those eighteen months whether I was committed to the path I was on, I would have said yes. I would have said it with conviction. But the pattern, looking at it now, is not someone discovering successively better paths. The pattern is someone who has trouble committing, dressing that difficulty up as ambition.

This is the part that hurts to write. The Generator instinct was not, on inspection, about Generator. It was the ninth option arriving exactly on cue.

The Question Underneath the Question

Once I forced myself to sit with that, the surface question dissolved.

Should I apply to a three month AI safety residency. That is not the actual question. The actual question is: what do I believe about the next ten years, and am I willing to act like I believe it.

If I think AI timelines are short, the optimal move is not a pre-packaged residency in Berkeley. The optimal move is cold-emailing the people doing the work, being useful in the room, taking unpaid weeks to prove I can produce. The thing about transformative technology windows is that the people who win them do not wait for an application portal to open. They show up uninvited and become indispensable. A three month program is the legible version of the move. It is not the move.

If I think AI timelines are long, then Welltower is obviously the right seat. Healthcare real estate at scale, an aging population, a balance sheet I can learn from, a city I want to live in. The thesis writes itself. It is the bet I already made, and the bet I would make again on the merits.

The only path that does not make sense, looking at it cold, is the hedge. The path where I take Welltower and apply to Generator on the side, and tell myself I am keeping my options open. That is not optionality. That is just a more sophisticated version of the same pattern that has been costing me real time for the last year and a half.

Ethan and Frank

I want to give credit where it is due, because the people who pushed back hardest on my framing were not the ones agreeing with me.

My friends Ethan and Frank are doing serious AI safety work right now. Neither of them did Generator. Neither of them did MATS. Neither of them is doing a PhD. They cold-emailed advisors until someone agreed to fund the work, and then they did the work. Their model is not the legible one. Their model is the one where you skip the application and write the email.

When I told them about Generator, the response was not "you should apply." The response was, more or less, "what do you actually want to be doing in six months, and who do you have to email today to be doing it." That is the right question. That is the only question.

I have used this model before. In college I wrote letters to Henry Kravis and Jamie Dimon. Kravis actually wrote back. I sat in his office. The rules-don't-apply frame is real, and it has worked in my life when I have used it. But it only works when you are using it to commit to something. Not when you are using it to generate the ninth option from a list of nine.

Where I Actually Am

I have not decided. That is the honest version of this essay.

I might still apply to a future Generator cohort. I might cold-email three researchers next week and find out what useful looks like. I might do neither and put my head down at Welltower in June and not think about any of this again until something else shows up to interrupt the commitment. The probability on each of those is real.

The cost of running nine paths in parallel is not that none of them are right. The cost is that you never get the depth that any single one of them would have produced. A wide shallow lake instead of a well.

The thing I want to do, and the only thing I think actually matters right now, is to stop running parallel processes. Pick the thing. Be wrong about it for twenty four months. Then reassess.

The version of me who picks a direction and stays on it for two years, even imperfectly, ends up further along than the version who keeps optimizing the picker.

The Welltower offer is on the table. June is six weeks away. The Generator deadline already passed.

I am going to stop refreshing the inbox.
