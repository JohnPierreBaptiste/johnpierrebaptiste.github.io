Institutional Capital Meets AI: What's Actually Happening

I spent last month sitting across the table from seven different allocators. Pension funds, endowments, a sovereign wealth fund that shall remain nameless. Every single one of them opened the conversation the same way: "We need more AI exposure." Not one of them could tell me what that meant.

This is the state of institutional capital in AI right now. Enormous conviction, almost zero specificity. And the gap between those two things is where most of the money is getting destroyed.

The Great Misallocation

Here's what I keep seeing. A large LP, let's say a $40 billion pension fund in the Midwest, decides they're underweight AI. Their investment committee has been reading the same McKinsey reports everyone else reads. They go to their existing GPs and say, essentially, "give us AI." The GP, who raised their fund to do enterprise SaaS or fintech or whatever their actual expertise is, suddenly rebrands half their pipeline as "AI-native." Everyone's happy. The allocation box gets checked. And the money flows into a series B for a company that bolted a ChatGPT wrapper onto an existing workflow tool and called it artificial intelligence.

I'm not exaggerating. I've seen the pitch decks.

The fundamental problem is that most institutional investors are using frameworks built for previous technology waves. They think about AI the way they thought about cloud in 2012 or mobile in 2009. Pick the layer of the stack you believe in, find the best team there, write a check. But AI doesn't work like cloud. The stack is still moving. The layers haven't settled. What looks like infrastructure today becomes a feature tomorrow, and what looks like a thin application layer today might be the entire platform in eighteen months.

Infrastructure: The Comfort Zone

Infrastructure is where most of the serious institutional money has gone, and for understandable reasons. It pattern-matches to what allocators already know. Chips, data centers, networking equipment. These are physical things with capex cycles and depreciation schedules. You can model them. You can visit the facility and kick the tires, literally.

NVIDIA's market cap tells you everything about how this trade has played out at the public level. But what's happening in private markets is more interesting and more worrying. I've tracked over $85 billion in announced commitments to AI infrastructure buildouts in the last fourteen months alone. Hyperscalers, sovereign funds, pension capital, all pouring into GPU clusters and custom silicon and the power generation needed to run it all.

Some of this is smart. The compute demand is real. Training runs aren't getting smaller. But there's a dangerous assumption buried in these allocations: that the current architecture is permanent. That transformers and attention mechanisms and the specific hardware optimized for them will look roughly the same in five years. I have a friend at a major chip design firm who says something that haunts me. "We're building cathedrals for a religion that might change its god."

That's not a reason to avoid infrastructure entirely. It is a reason to be very precise about what kind of infrastructure you're buying and on what timeline you need it to pay back.

The Application Layer Mirage

Now let's talk about the other side, because this is where the real confusion lives.

Most VCs will tell you the application layer is where the value accrues. They'll cite Salesforce and Workday and the whole SaaS playbook. Build on top of the platform, own the customer relationship, compound via switching costs. And they're not wrong about the historical pattern. They might be very wrong about whether it applies here.

The problem with AI applications is that the moat question is genuinely unsettled. I've watched three companies in the last year build what looked like defensible AI products, get real enterprise traction, hit $20M ARR, and then watch their core value proposition get absorbed into the foundation model itself. One of them was in my own portfolio. It hurt.

The lesson wasn't that application layer investing is dead. The lesson was that the boundary between "model capability" and "application" is not fixed. It's a moving frontier. And if you're building your application right on top of that frontier, you're building on sand.

Where the real application value lives

The companies I'm most excited about aren't the ones with the cleverest prompt engineering or the most sophisticated RAG pipelines. They're the ones that have figured out something about a specific domain that has nothing to do with the model. Proprietary data flywheels. Regulatory expertise that takes years to build. Workflow integration so deep that ripping it out would mean rearchitecting the customer's entire operation.

I'll give you a concrete example without naming the company. There's a team I've been watching in specialty insurance. They're using AI, sure. But their actual advantage is that they've spent three years digitizing and structuring claims data that existed only in filing cabinets and legacy mainframes. The AI is the engine, but the fuel is irreplaceable. No foundation model update threatens them because the model was never their moat. The data was.

This is the pattern I keep telling allocators to look for. Don't ask "is this an AI company?" Ask "would this company's advantage survive if the entire AI stack got ten times better overnight?" If the answer is yes, you might have something.

What LPs Are Getting Wrong

Let me be direct about the three biggest mistakes I see institutional allocators making right now.

1. Treating AI as a sector

AI is not a sector. It's a capability shift. Allocating to "AI" makes about as much sense as allocating to "electricity" in 1920. Yes, the companies building generators and running copper wire made money. But the real wealth creation happened in every other industry that electricity transformed. Refrigeration. Manufacturing. Entertainment. The investors who won weren't the ones who said "I'm long electricity." They were the ones who understood which specific industries would be remade and how.

When I see an LP with an "AI allocation bucket," I know they're going to misallocate. The best AI investments in 2026 might not look like AI investments at all. They might look like logistics companies or legal services firms or biotech platforms that happen to be rebuilt from the ground up with intelligence at the core.

2. Confusing revenue with defensibility

This one is epidemic. I keep hearing allocators say things like "but they have $50 million in ARR" as if that settles the question. Revenue is not a moat. Revenue in AI is especially not a moat, because the cost to replicate many AI applications is collapsing faster than anyone's financial models can account for.

I sat on a panel last quarter with a GP who proudly described a portfolio company doing $30M ARR in AI-powered content generation. I asked him what happens when the next model update makes their specific fine-tuning irrelevant. He said, "We'll cross that bridge when we come to it." That bridge is about six months away and the water is rising.

The allocators who are getting this right are the ones asking a different question. Not "how much revenue?" but "what does the revenue quality look like in a world where model capabilities improve by 10x?" Some businesses get stronger in that world. Most get weaker. You'd be amazed how few people in institutional investing are even running that thought experiment.

3. Overweighting the GP's AI narrative and underweighting their actual track record of technical judgment

Every fund is an AI fund now. I get it. But when I'm evaluating a GP's ability to invest in AI, I don't want to hear their macro thesis on artificial general intelligence. I want to know if they've ever made a technical bet that went against consensus and turned out right. I want to know if they have anyone on the team who has actually shipped production ML systems. Not advised. Not consulted. Shipped.

The number of funds claiming AI expertise that don't have a single person with real technical depth is staggering. And LPs keep falling for it because the slides are beautiful and the narrative is compelling and who wants to admit they can't tell the difference between genuine technical insight and sophisticated storytelling?

Where I Think This Goes

I'll tell you what I think happens over the next two years, and I'm writing this down so you can hold me to it.

First, the infrastructure overbuild will become obvious by late 2026. Not because demand for compute decreases, but because efficiency gains in inference and training will outpace the buildout timeline. Some of these massive GPU installations will be underutilized. The smart infrastructure investors are already thinking about optionality and repurposing. The dumb ones are going to be holding very expensive, very depreciating assets.

Second, there will be a brutal shakeout in the application layer. My rough estimate is that 60-70% of companies currently described as "AI-native applications" will either fail, get acqui-hired for their talent, or pivot to something that looks nothing like their current pitch. The ones that survive will be the ones I described earlier. The ones with domain depth that transcends the model layer.

Third, and this is the one I'm most convicted on, the biggest AI-driven returns for institutional investors won't come from AI-labeled investments at all. They'll come from traditional businesses in healthcare, financial services, logistics, and manufacturing that figure out how to reorganize around AI capabilities before their competitors do. The LPs who build frameworks for identifying these companies, regardless of what sector bucket they sit in, will dramatically outperform.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Here's the thing nobody in institutional investing wants to hear. The skills required to evaluate AI investments well are fundamentally different from the skills most allocators have. Understanding whether a foundation model startup has genuine technical differentiation requires a kind of knowledge that you can't get from a two-day AI bootcamp at Wharton. Evaluating whether an AI application has durable value requires understanding both the technology trajectory and the domain it serves at a level of specificity that most generalist investors simply don't have.

This isn't a criticism. It's a structural observation. The institutional investment ecosystem was built for a world where the key differentiators were financial analysis, relationship access, and pattern matching against historical outcomes. AI investing requires all of those things plus genuine technical literacy. Not expertise, necessarily. But literacy. The ability to ask the right questions and smell when the answers don't make sense.

The allocators I respect most are the ones being honest about this gap. They're hiring differently. They're restructuring their diligence processes. They're spending time with engineers, not just founders and CFOs. They're building the muscle instead of faking it.

Because the worst possible outcome isn't missing the AI wave. It's catching a version of it that was never real to begin with. And right now, with this much capital chasing this much narrative, that's exactly what's happening to a lot of very sophisticated investors who should know better.

The money is moving. The question is whether it's moving toward actual value creation or toward the most convincing story about value creation. In my experience, the gap between those two things has never been wider.
